Lesson Material: 19th Century British Homes

A Case Study in AI-Assisted ESL Curriculum Design

Source Text

In the 19th century, the Industrial Revolution changed British life forever. Wealthy families in cities lived in large houses filled with expensive decorations, big furniture, and colorful carpets, which they used to show off their success. In contrast, working-class families lived in tiny, cramped terraced houses, often with only one or two rooms. Despite these physical differences, both groups saw the home as a "sanctuary"—a place of safety away from the noisy, dirty cities. While wealthy families achieved comfort through material possessions, poor families found security by sitting together around a small fire. Ultimately, the true meaning of "home" as a place of family protection remained the same for both classes.

Part 1: The Ordinary Approach

The Prompt
"Here is a text about 19th-century British homes. Generate 5 reading comprehension questions and an answer key for my ESL class."
AI Output (The "Sentence Lifting" Trap)
1. What did wealthy families have in their rooms?
Expensive decorations, big furniture, and colorful carpets.
2. Where did people move during the Industrial Revolution?
To busy cities.
3. How many rooms did poor houses have?
One or two rooms.
4. How did poor families find comfort?
By sitting together around a small fire.
5. Did social class change the meaning of home?
No, it was a place of safety for both groups.
Pedagogical Critique: These are "Right There" questions. A student can answer these by scanning the text for keywords and copying the words directly, without ever processing the concept of the Industrial Revolution or the differing definitions of "security."

Part 2: The Enriched Approach

The Prompt
"Act as an expert ESL curriculum designer. Review the text on '19th Century British Homes.' Create a 3-part worksheet that utilizes Sample-questions-from-Blooms for cognitive escalation and Critical Thinking Across The Curriculum for deep synthesis.

Create 5 questions based on Sample-questions-from-Blooms, escalating from 'Remembering' to 'Evaluating.'

CRITICAL: The model answers must be completely rephrased (B1+ level). Students must not be able to answer by 'sentence lifting' directly from the text. Require them to infer and contrast.

Based on the Discussion_Toolkit principles, add a 4th part: 3 discussion questions that connect the themes of 'home as a sanctuary' to the students' own lived experiences."
AI Output (The Advanced Pedagogical Result)

Part A: Bloom’s Taxonomy Questions (Synthesis & Evaluation)

(Understanding): Explain in your own words how the Industrial Revolution forced people to change their idea of "home."
Model Answer: Before the revolution, homes were likely workplaces; afterwards, they became private, quiet spaces used to escape the polluted city environment.
(Analyzing): Contrast the ways wealthy and working-class families achieved "coziness." How did their financial status dictate their method of finding security?
Model Answer: Wealthy families used material goods to create comfort, while working-class families, lacking resources, relied on the physical closeness of family members near a fire.
(Evaluating): To what extent does the author suggest that social class was irrelevant to the "true meaning of home"?
Model Answer: The author suggests class was irrelevant because, regardless of whether a house was large or tiny, it functioned as the same psychological tool for family safety.

Part B: Critical Thinking & Personalization

(Personalization): The text defines home as a "sanctuary." Does your own home environment function as a sanctuary from your daily stress? Why or why not?
(Critical Evaluation): Do you agree that "material possessions" (like the carpets in the text) are necessary to make a house a "home"?
(Idiomatic Connection): Using the target vocabulary, have you ever felt "green with envy" regarding someone else's living situation? Explain your perspective.

Key Takeaways for Curriculum Design

  • The Ordinary Approach Encourages passive, superficial scanning. It tests vision, not intellect.
  • The Enriched Approach Forces students to synthesize (rephrasing the answer in their own words), evaluate (judging the author's argument), and connect (bridging history with their personal worldview). This turns the text into a "living" debate rather than a static block of information.
